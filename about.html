About

My background is a bit like bread

Much like flour, Physics is the structure of everything I do and feeds my Computer Science, the yeast. Math, which translates the science to my code, binds these two together, like fat. Finally, Communication, like sugar, makes the whole thing sweet and palatable. Right now, at UNC-Chapel Hill, I combine all these ingredients to bake my bread: non-equilibrium statistical mechanics. Mmmmm, delicious!


Specifically, I aim to understand the role of self-propulsion on collective behavior. To give you an example, typically, we think of physics as occuring in a distant galaxy or locked away in a lab. However, what I study is a system you see everyday: it's you! How does an individual's ability to move and interact with others influence the crowd's collective behavior?


To answer this question, I simulate hundreds of thousands of self-propelling particles with C++. As you can see from the video, this system is inherently messy and out of equilibrium: everything constantly moves and never reaches a final resting point, making analysis particularly challenging. Using algorithms I develop in Python, I boil down big data to simple relationships that explain how the individual's properties determine the group's behavior.


So, my background in Computational Physics certainly comes in handy for research, but, at the same time, it has lent itself to every aspect of my graduate career. I've utilized my Software Development skills to design this website, my Data Science skills to create a user database and analytics system for my university's makerspace, and my Communication to not only teach science to high schoolers but also for presenting bills as a senator in student government. My computational physics background has shaped me to be well-rounded, much like a good, well-risen loaf of bread.

Teaching and Mentoring

My B.S. is in Physics and Mathematics (University of Wisconsin-Madison 2018). In 3 years of undergraduate research, I designed a Python model to simulate fusion plasma energy emissions and applied it to develop a x-ray detector for my senior thesis. As a sophomore in college, I had never dreamed of accomplishing such lofty goals, in fact, upon starting research, I doubted the direction I was heading. What do I want to do as a career? Was physics right for me? How can I ever build a computational model when I can't speak in 0's and 1's! Research and programming were completely foreign to me. I was intimidated, but I learned the value of having a guide. Thanks to my research mentor's support and friendship, I developed not only my passion for Computational Physics but also my desire to be a mentor as she had been to me.


With this newfound passion, I pursued my PhD in Materials Science (University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill 2024) supervised by Daphne Klotsa. As a graduate student, I made it a priority to translate my goal of mentoring others into action. At the end of my first year, I served as the Senator for my department in the Graduate and Professional Student Government (GPSG) where I organized regular town halls to share notable opportunities and changes at UNC to my constituents. As a teaching assistant, I mentored three small groups of students through semester-long research projects, organized and taught lectures on computational modeling, and held regular 1-on-1 tutoring sessions for students seeking supplementary support. Furthermore, I mentored three high school students from across the world on research projects for the Junior Science and Humanities Symposium (JSHS) and the International Science and Engineering Fair (ISEF) where one of my students won regionals and competed at the international level for both.
